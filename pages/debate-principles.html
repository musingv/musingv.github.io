Principles of Engagement

Here’s a quick overview of how I aspire to interact with others and their ideas when I write.

Steelman, Not Strawman

To the extent I am able, I want to present and interact with the strongest version of an argument, especially when I disagree with it.

Backtrack Readily

There’s a lot I don’t know. Suppose that I only write about what I know well, and I’m right 95% of the time about what I know well (humor me, here). That would mean that if I wrote once a week, I would be completely wrong about 2 or 3 whole posts after a year.

That’s a sobering thought. So a few goals:

  1. Lightly hold on to initial conclusions, especially about news and current affairs.
  2. Expect to be wrong. Do not let the publicity of my statements prevent me from changing my stance to a more reasonable conclusion.

Understand and Mull

For the same reasons that I want to backtrack readily, I want to do my best that I understand where others are coming from – and the implications of such views.

Reality, Not Preconceptions, Cliches, or Marketing

Reality is messy and convoluted at times. But I want to deal with the world as it is, not maintain a facade. It’s easy to repeat a notion (“Of course churches are safe places for people.”) without looking around and asking questions like “How would I know if my church wasn’t a safe place for some people?” If God is really the source of truth, if he is really big enough to deal with the brokenness of the world, if Jesus really is the savior of the world, then surely we don’t need to deal in platitudes, right?

The Lunch Test

I’d like people to be willing to have lunch with me after I’ve interacted with their content. Even if, and perhaps especially if, we have fundamental disagreements1 2.

  1. While there’s certainly a place for polemic denouncements, my take is that a villein should generally leave those to others. Beyond that, overly quick or broad denouncements often seem to solidify resistance rather than bridge gaps.
